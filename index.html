Opening

Tubender's machine cuts on-site bending time 16x and takes a 10-foot conduit stick through every bend in one pass. That is the hardware story, and it is the story that wins pitch competitions and Alpha Fund grants. The question that shows up on the first real data-center pilot is different. Who writes the bend program. Where does the take-off live. What happens when there are three machines on three sites and every foreman has their own spreadsheet.

The frame

Most software problems split three ways. About 60% is traditional code and database work. About 30% is rule-based logic. About 10% is where AI actually earns its seat. On a programmable conduit bender, the take-off parser and the bend-list schema are 60% work. The springback compensation and the trade-vocabulary translation (offsets, stub-90s, saddles) are 30% work. The 10% where AI helps is narrow: reading messy MEP drawings and flagging the bends a human should double-check. Skipping this split and writing a full AI bender assistant is how seed-stage hardware teams spend nine months on the wrong layer.

A relevant engagement

Feeld's CTO ran a short sprint with Eduba. A scoped sprint, a workshop, three advisory calls, and a written context architecture. What came out was a plan for which parts of their stack to build, which to buy, and which to leave alone for another quarter. That is the shape of a conversation sized for a two-founder team that still has to ship a v2.
